# Matt Paulin Writing Voice Guide

## Core Identity

Matt writes like a product leader talking to a smart colleague over coffee. He's direct, personal, and grounded in real experience. He teaches by sharing what he's actually done — not by lecturing from theory.

---

## Tone & Attitude

- **Conversational but thoughtful.** Not academic, not corporate. Reads like someone thinking out loud with a pen in hand.
- **Confident without being preachy.** He states what he believes clearly ("This is my motto for AI") but doesn't oversell or moralize.
- **Honest about limits.** He'll say "To be honest, I've never personally worked on an RL7 or higher project" or "It wasn't perfect — but it was *alive*." He doesn't pretend to have all the answers.
- **Quietly enthusiastic.** Excitement comes through in what he chooses to write about, not in exclamation marks or hype words. "Spike 7 was an absolute adventure" is about as high as it gets.

---

## Sentence Style

- **Short sentences do real work.** "Ten minutes, tops." / "Speed wasn't the hard part. Trust was." / "That, to me, is the heart of innovation."
- **Mix of short punches and longer, more flowing thoughts.** He doesn't write in one gear. A tight declarative sentence is often followed by a more reflective one.
- **Bold text for key takeaways** — used sparingly, like highlighting the sentence you'd want someone to remember.
- **Dashes and colons for pacing** — he uses them to set up a punchline or reframe a thought mid-sentence.

---

## Point of View

- **First person, always.** "I believe", "I've found", "My company", "My son and I". He's present in his own writing.
- **"You" to include the reader.** "Look around you right now at every product or tool you're using." "You can't have a meaningful conversation about software development without eventually asking..."
- **"We" when talking about shared team experiences.** "We tried many standard approaches." "We added a Monday/Friday cadence."

---

## How He Explains Things

- **Analogies from the physical world.** Claude Code is "like a 3D printer for software." Cursor is "like a Dremel." A team leader tends to the ecosystem "like a gardener." A chef still has to taste the food.
- **Frameworks come from experience, then get named.** He'll describe what happened, then give it a label — not the other way around. The readiness levels came from a friend; he explains how he uses them. "Automate and Elevate" is introduced as "my motto" after he's already shown why it matters.
- **Credits others naturally.** "These readiness levels come originally from my friend Shane." He names people, links to their work, and situates his thinking inside a community.
- **Concrete examples before abstractions.** He'll talk about the mom doing meal planning before he introduces the concierge strategy. He describes what the team actually did before naming the principle.

---

## Structure

- **Sections with clear headers**, but the writing within them is flowing, not bullet-point-only.
- **Bullet lists for practical lists** (tools, steps, examples) — not for every paragraph.
- **Narrative momentum.** Blog posts feel like they're going somewhere. He opens with a hook or personal statement, builds through examples, and lands on a takeaway.
- **No rigid template.** Each post has its own shape. Some are reflective essays, some are how-to guides, some are project logs. The structure serves the content, not the reverse.

---

## What He Does NOT Do

- **No corporate buzzwords.** He doesn't "leverage synergies" or "drive innovation." He says what he means plainly.
- **No hollow superlatives.** He doesn't call things "game-changing" or "revolutionary." If something is impressive, he shows you why through the story.
- **No detached third-person voice.** He doesn't write "This case reflects how I approach product leadership" followed by a generic bullet list. That's resume-speak, not Matt.
- **No formulaic repetition.** He doesn't use the same section headers across every piece. Each article has its own structure based on what it needs.
- **No false polish.** His writing has a human texture — the occasional rough edge, the aside, the admission. This is intentional. Over-polished writing feels corporate and hollow.
- **No "The Real Problem (Not the Obvious One)" as a repeated rhetorical device.** He frames problems naturally through storytelling, not through templated contrast patterns.

---

## The "ChatGPT" Patterns to Avoid

These are the specific patterns present in the current case studies that clash with Matt's voice:

1. **Identical structure across every piece.** Every case study uses the same sections in the same order (Situation / Real Problem / Constraints / Key Decisions / Outcomes / What This Says About Me). Matt's blog posts each have unique structures.
2. **Detached, resume-style closing lists.** "I design systems, not features / I optimize for human behavior, not ideal workflows" — this reads like LinkedIn copy, not like Matt talking.
3. **Passive voice and removed perspective.** "Product decisions consistently favored stability over experimentation" — Matt would say "We chose stability over experimentation" or "I pushed for stability."
4. **No stories, no people, no texture.** The case studies have no anecdotes, no named colleagues, no moments of doubt or surprise. Matt's blog posts are full of these.
5. **Over-structured bullet points replacing narrative.** The case studies are almost entirely headers and bullets. Matt uses bullets for lists, but wraps them in flowing paragraphs that carry the reader forward.
6. **Hollow gravitas.** Phrases like "product decisions directly affected public safety, equity, and public trust" — technically true but reads like a press release. Matt would ground this in a specific moment or decision.

---

## Voice Checklist (for rewriting)

When rewriting content in Matt's voice, check:

- [ ] Is there a first-person perspective throughout?
- [ ] Are there specific stories, moments, or people named?
- [ ] Does the structure serve this particular piece (not a template)?
- [ ] Are there analogies or metaphors from the real world?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment of honesty or vulnerability?
- [ ] Are bold statements earned through context, not asserted?
- [ ] Does it sound like someone you'd want to grab a beer with?
- [ ] Would Matt actually say this sentence out loud?
